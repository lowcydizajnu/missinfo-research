title: "Adding Conditional Logic Rules to Your Study"
sidebarTitle: "Conditional Logic"
description: "Write deterministic if→then routing rules that fire on top of random assignment to skip parts, end sessions, or hide questions based on participant data."
---

Conditional logic lets you add deterministic branching rules to your study. Where random assignment determines which content arm or metric condition a participant receives, conditional logic lets you act on what a participant has actually **done or answered** — routing them to a different part, ending their session early, or hiding a question based on a specific trigger. Rules are defined in the **Configurator** tab and are evaluated at defined moments during the participant's journey.

Conditional logic and random assignment work together: random assignment still runs first, and your rules layer on top of it. A participant assigned to Arm B might still be routed past a part if a conditional rule fires based on their demographic answer.

## When Rules Fire

Rules are evaluated at three moments in the study flow:

<CardGroup cols={3}>
  <Card title="After Demographics" icon="address-card">
    Rules are checked immediately after the participant submits their demographic responses. Use this moment to screen out ineligible participants or route different demographic groups to different parts.
  </Card>
  <Card title="After a Part" icon="list-check">
    Rules are checked after the participant completes each experiment part. Use this moment to branch based on how the participant reacted to the content in that part.
  </Card>
  <Card title="After a Reaction" icon="thumbs-up">
    Rules are checked immediately when a participant applies a reaction to a specific post. Use this moment for fine-grained real-time routing based on individual post interactions.
  </Card>
</CardGroup>

## Trigger Types

Each rule begins with a **trigger** — the condition that must be true for the rule's action to fire.

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="Demographic answer">
    Fires when a participant's answer to a specific demographic question matches a value you specify. For example: *if `age` is less than 18* or *if `residence` equals `outside_target_country`*. Use this trigger for eligibility screening or audience-stratified routing.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Post-question answer">
    Fires when a participant's answer to a question attached to a specific post matches a value you specify. For example: *if the answer to the credibility question on Post 3 is `not_credible`*. Use this trigger to route participants based on their belief judgements.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Reaction">
    Fires when a participant applies a specific reaction (like, dislike, share, or flag) to a specific post. For example: *if participant flags Post 7*. Use this trigger for engagement-based routing.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Assigned condition">
    Fires based on the participant's randomly assigned style condition (`A` or `B`) or metric condition key. For example: *if `style_condition` is `A`*. Use this trigger to add deterministic actions on top of the random assignment — for example, skipping a part that is only relevant for one arm.
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>

## Action Types

Each rule ends with an **action** — what happens when the trigger fires.

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="Skip a part">
    The participant bypasses the specified experiment part entirely. Posts in that part are not shown and no reactions are recorded. The participant proceeds directly to the next non-skipped part or screen.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="End the study">
    The participant's session is terminated and they are taken to the debrief screen (or a custom exit screen if configured). No further posts, questions, or parts are shown. The session is marked as completed up to the point where the rule fired.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Go to a part">
    The participant is redirected to a specific experiment part, skipping any parts in between. Unlike "Skip a part" (which removes one part), "Go to a part" can jump forward multiple parts in one action.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Hide a question">
    A specific question — either a demographic question or a post-attached question — is hidden from this participant. The question is not shown and no response is recorded for it. Use this to conditionally suppress irrelevant follow-up questions.
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>

## Adding a Rule

<Steps>
  <Step title="Open the Configurator tab">
    Navigate to your study and select the **Configurator** tab.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Expand the Conditional Logic section">
    Scroll to **Conditional Logic** near the bottom of the Configurator and expand it.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Click Add Rule">
    A new rule card appears. Each rule has a trigger block and an action block.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Define the trigger">
    Select the trigger type from the dropdown and fill in the trigger parameters — the question or post to watch, and the value or reaction to match.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Define the action">
    Select the action type and, where applicable, the target part or question.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Set when the rule is evaluated">
    Specify the evaluation moment: after demographics, after a specific part, or after a reaction on a specific post. This controls when the platform checks whether the trigger condition is met.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Save and validate">
    Click **Save**. The platform validates all rules for consistency — for example, checking that referenced parts and questions exist. Validation errors are shown inline and must be resolved before the study can be published.
  </Step>
</Steps>

## Validation

When you save the Configurator, all conditional logic rules are validated automatically. Common validation errors include:

- Referencing a part or question that has been deleted
- Creating a circular route (Part A → go to Part B → go to Part A)
- Defining a trigger on a post that is not assigned to any active part
- Using a metric condition key that does not exist

Fix all validation errors before publishing. A study with invalid rules cannot be activated.

## Rules in Preview Mode

<Note>
  Conditional logic rules do **not** fire when you preview your study using the builder's preview function. Preview mode is designed to let you check the appearance and flow of your content, not to simulate the full branching logic. Always test conditional routing by running a real (or internal) session with a test participant code.
</Note>

## Example Use Cases

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="Age-based screen-out">
    **Trigger**: Demographic answer — `age` is less than 18  
    **Action**: End the study  
    **Fires**: After demographics  

    Participants who report being under 18 are immediately routed to the debrief screen and their session is closed. This is evaluated after the demographics block, so ineligible participants never see any experiment content.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Condition-based part skip">
    **Trigger**: Assigned condition — `style_condition` equals `A`  
    **Action**: Skip Part 3  
    **Fires**: After Part 2  

    Participants in Arm A skip Part 3 entirely. This is useful when Part 3 contains a task only relevant to the Arm B experience — for example, a follow-up block of debunking messages that you only want Arm B to see.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Reaction-based routing">
    **Trigger**: Reaction — participant shares Post 5  
    **Action**: Go to Part 4  
    **Fires**: After reaction on Post 5  

    If a participant shares a specific post, they are routed immediately to Part 4, bypassing any remaining posts in the current part. Use cautiously — this creates a non-uniform experience that can complicate analysis.
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>

## A Note on Screen-Out Rules

<Warning>
  Rules that end the study based on a participant's own answers — such as screening out by age, political affiliation, or any demographic attribute — **change the composition of your analysed sample**. Participants who are screened out are not randomly distributed across your conditions; they are systematically removed before they encounter any stimuli. This is appropriate for strict eligibility criteria (e.g. minimum age requirements) but can introduce bias if applied to variables that may correlate with your outcome measures. Document any screen-out rules in your pre-registration and methods section, and report the number of participants removed at each screening step.
</Warning>
